⚠️ What Happens If Left Uncontrolled — Know Your Risk

👁️ Blindness

Diabetic retinopathy. High glucose destroys retinal blood vessels. Begins silently after 5–10 years.

🫘 Kidney Failure

Diabetic nephropathy. Each HbA1c rise increases kidney disease risk by 10–15%. Requires dialysis.

❤️ Heart Attack

Diabetes + smoking = 4x cardiac risk. 2/3 of T2D deaths are cardiovascular.

🦶 Foot Amputation

Neuropathy + poor circulation from smoking. Wounds stop healing. Can require amputation.

🧠 Stroke

High glucose + smoking damages blood vessels to the brain. Risk multiplied with both.

🫀 Liver Cirrhosis

NAFLD → NASH → Fibrosis → Cirrhosis. Diabetes + obesity accelerates progression.

💊 Insulin Dependence

Uncontrolled T2D for 5+ years often requires daily insulin injections. Still preventable now.

🩹 HS Worsening

Metabolic syndrome drives HS flare-ups. Diabetes makes wound healing worse after abscesses.

🦋 Myxedema (Severe Hypothyroid Emergency)

Untreated severe hypothyroidism (TSH 150) can rarely progress to myxedema coma — low body temperature, confusion, slow heart rate. Levothyroxine treatment prevents this entirely.

❤️ Heart Strain from Low Thyroid

Severe hypothyroidism raises LDL/triglycerides and can slow the heart rate, compounding your existing cardiovascular risk from diabetes and smoking.

⏰

How Levothyroxine Must Be Taken (This Matters a Lot)

  • Take on an empty stomach, first thing in the morning, 30–60 minutes before any food, tea, or other medicine
  • Do not take Calcium, Iron, or Magnesium supplements within 4 hours of levothyroxine — they block its absorption
  • Do not stop or change the dose yourself — it's adjusted slowly based on repeat TSH tests
  • Energy, weight, and mood usually start improving within a few weeks, but full correction can take a few months

🔗 How This Connects to Everything Else

Thyroid hormone controls your metabolic rate. When it's this low, your body burns energy more slowly, which can: raise LDL and triglycerides, make weight loss harder, worsen fatigue, and even push up liver enzymes like ALT/AST. Some of what looked like "just metabolic syndrome" — the high cholesterol, the stubborn weight, the tiredness — may partly be this untreated thyroid. Starting levothyroxine and bringing TSH back to normal should make your diabetes, lipids, and energy levels easier to manage, not harder.

⚠️ Symptoms to Watch For

Common symptoms of severe hypothyroidism include: constant tiredness, feeling unusually cold, dry skin, hair thinning, constipation, slow heart rate, puffiness around the face/eyes, and weight gain. If you notice these, they fit the lab picture and should improve once treatment starts.

🆘

Rare Emergency Signs — Go to ER If Present

Severe, prolonged, untreated hypothyroidism can rarely progress to myxedema coma: extreme cold intolerance, confusion or extreme drowsiness, very slow pulse, or significant swelling of the face/legs. This is rare, but if any of these appear, go to an emergency room immediately rather than waiting for a routine appointment.

🩺 What to Expect From Treatment
  • Levothyroxine (e.g. Eltroxin, Thyrox in Pakistan) is the standard first-line treatment — a single daily tablet that replaces the hormone your thyroid isn't making enough of.
  • Take it on an empty stomach, first thing in the morning, with water, then wait 30–60 minutes before eating or taking other medicines (especially calcium, iron, or antacids — they block absorption).
  • Your doctor will usually start at a low-to-moderate dose and recheck TSH every 6–8 weeks, adjusting the dose until TSH normalises — this can take a few months. Don't expect an instant fix.
  • Do not stop or change the dose yourself once started, even if you feel better — thyroid dosing is fine-tuned by blood tests, not by symptoms alone.
  • Once TSH is stable, it's typically checked every 6–12 months long-term.

🩺 What HS Is and How to Manage It
⚠️

Diagnosis: Hidradenitis Suppurativa — Not Poor Hygiene

HS is a chronic inflammatory disease of the follicular portion of the hair follicle unit. Painful, pus-filled nodules in armpits, thighs, groin, and buttocks = classic HS presentation. It is NOT caused by poor hygiene. It is a medical disease that requires medical treatment.

  • See a dermatologist — ask for "Hidradenitis Suppurativa" — at CMH GRW or DHQ. First-line treatment: tetracycline antibiotics (doxycycline 100 mg twice daily for 3 months).
  • Do NOT squeeze, pop, or drain lesions yourself — this spreads infection deeper and causes permanent scarring.
  • Weight loss is the #1 lifestyle treatment — every 5 kg lost reduces friction, sweat, and inflammatory cytokines. Studies show significant flare reduction after 10% weight loss.
  • Quitting smoking is critical — smoking is one of the 3 major HS triggers (alongside obesity and genetics).
  • Wear loose, light cotton clothing — friction triggers flares. Synthetic fabric = worse. Cotton = better.
